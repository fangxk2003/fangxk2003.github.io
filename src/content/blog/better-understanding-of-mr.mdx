## Build an Intuitive Grasp of Time

Under a 1.5 T $B_0$ field, the Larmor frequency of protons is $\omega_{H^+} = \gamma_{H^+} B_0$. Using $\gamma_{H^+} = 42.58$ MHz/T gives 63.87 MHz, so one cycle is 15.66 ns.

Let's look at the time of a $90^\circ$ RF pulse. The exact duration of a 90-degree pulse is governed by a fundamental relationship between the applied RF magnetic field $B_1$ and the gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma$ of the nucleus being targeted:

$$
\gamma B_1 t_p=\frac{\pi}{2}
$$

$B_1$ is usually 2-20 uT for standard sequences in clinical MRI (1.5 T/3 T); let's take 10 uT. We can calculate that $t_p$ is 3.69 ms. That's $10^5$ times spin cycle.

Let's see T1 and T2 for common tissues (ms at 1.5 T):

| Tissue | T1 (ms) | T2 (ms) | MRI appearance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) | 2500-4500 | 200-600 | T1 dark, T2 bright |
| Gray matter | 900-950 | 100 | T1 gray, T2 light gray |
| White matter | 780-800 | 90 | T1 light gray, T2 dark gray |
| Fat (adipose) | 250-260 | 60-100 | T1 bright, T2 intermediate |
| Myocardium (heart) | 950-1050 | 50-55 | Intermediate on T1/T2 |
| Muscle | 850-900 | 35-50 | T1/T2 dark gray |
| Liver | 500-600 | 40-45 | T1 medium gray |
| Kidney | 600-700 | 55-60 | Variable contrast |

That's approximately 100 times RF pulse duration.

## 90-degree Flip and 180-degree Flip in Spin Echo

Now that an tuitive grasp of time is builded. Let's look at some technical details.

![spin-echo-sequence](/images/better-understanding-of-mr/spin-echo-sequence.png)

The following GIF is from [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_echo)

![HahnEcho_GWM](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/HahnEcho_GWM.gif)

It's great illustration but has some small mistakes.

Classic spin echo generate T2 contrast with long TR and long TE. And gradient echo(GRE) generate T2* contrast.


| Feature | T2 (True T2) | T2* (T2-Star) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Causes | Random, microscopic spin-spin interactions. | Spin-spin interactions + magnetic field inhomogeneities. |
| Speed | Slower decay rate. | Accelerated, much faster decay rate. |
| Can it be reversed? | No. It is fundamentally random. | Yes. The static field errors can be corrected. |

$$
\frac{1}{T_2^*}=\frac{1}{T_2}+\frac{1}{T_{2,inhom}}
$$


After the 90-degree flip and before the 180-degree flip, both $T_2$ and $T_{2,inhom}$ are effective, so it's equal to a T2* relaxation.

Microscopically, for each proton:

$$
\Delta \phi_{inhom}(t)=\Delta \omega_{inhom} \cdot t \\
\Delta \phi_{spin-spin}(t)=\Delta \omega_{spin-spin} \cdot t
$$

Macroscopically, for the magnetic moment:

$$
M_{xy}(t)=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2}}\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_{2,inhom}}}=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2^*}}
$$

After the 180-degree flip, $T_2$ is still dephasing the magnetic momentum, but $T_{2,inhom}$ is giving a recovery effect(like the widely used shuttle sprint example).

Microscopically, for each proton:

$$
\Delta \phi_{inhom}(t)=\Delta \omega_{inhom} \cdot |t-TE| \\
\Delta \phi_{spin-spin}(t)=\Delta \omega_{spin-spin} \cdot t
$$

Macroscopically, for the magnetic moment:

$$
M_{xy}(t)=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2}}\cdot e^{-\frac{|t-TE|}{T_{2,inhom}}}
$$
