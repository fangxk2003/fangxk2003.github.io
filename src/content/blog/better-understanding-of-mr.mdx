## Build an Intuitive Grasp of Time

Under a 1.5 T $B_0$ field, the proton Larmor frequency is $f_{H^+} = \bar{\gamma}_{H^+} B_0$. Using $\bar{\gamma}_{H^+} = 42.58$ MHz/T gives 63.87 MHz, so one cycle takes 15.66 ns.

Now compare that with the duration of a $90^\circ$ RF pulse. The flip angle is determined by the applied RF magnetic field $B_1$ and the angular gyromagnetic ratio $\gamma = 2\pi\bar{\gamma}$:

$$
\gamma B_1 t_p=\frac{\pi}{2}
$$

For standard clinical MRI sequences at 1.5 T or 3 T, $B_1$ is often on the order of 2-20 uT. If we take $B_1 = 10$ uT, then $t_p \approx 0.59$ ms. That is roughly $4\times10^4$ Larmor cycles.

Now compare this with typical T1 and T2 values for common tissues at 1.5 T:

| Tissue | T1 (ms) | T2 (ms) | MRI appearance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) | 2500-4500 | 200-600 | T1 dark, T2 bright |
| Gray matter | 900-950 | 100 | T1 gray, T2 light gray |
| White matter | 780-800 | 90 | T1 light gray, T2 dark gray |
| Fat (adipose) | 250-260 | 60-100 | T1 bright, T2 intermediate |
| Myocardium (heart) | 950-1050 | 50-55 | Intermediate on T1/T2 |
| Muscle | 850-900 | 35-50 | T1/T2 dark gray |
| Liver | 500-600 | 40-45 | T1 medium gray |
| Kidney | 600-700 | 55-60 | Variable contrast |

Even the shorter T2 values are tens to hundreds of times longer than the RF pulse. This separation of time scales is one reason we can often think of an RF pulse as a short, controlled rotation, and then think about relaxation afterward.

## 90-degree Flip and 180-degree Flip in Spin Echo

With that time scale in mind, let's look at a few technical details.

![spin-echo-sequence](/images/better-understanding-of-mr/spin-echo-sequence.png)

The following GIF is from [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_echo):

![HahnEcho_GWM](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/HahnEcho_GWM.gif)

It is a helpful illustration, but there is one detail worth being precise about.

A classic spin echo sequence with long TR and long TE emphasizes T2 contrast. A gradient echo (GRE) sequence, by contrast, usually preserves T2* contrast because it does not use a 180-degree RF pulse to refocus static field inhomogeneity.


| Feature | T2 | T2* |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Cause | Random microscopic spin-spin interactions | Spin-spin interactions plus magnetic field inhomogeneity |
| Decay rate | Slower | Faster |
| Can it be reversed? | No. The phase loss is microscopic and random. | Partly. Static field errors can be refocused. |

$$
\frac{1}{T_2^*}=\frac{1}{T_2}+\frac{1}{T_{2,inhom}}
$$


After the 90-degree pulse and before the 180-degree pulse, both true $T_2$ decay and inhomogeneity-driven dephasing are active, so the signal decays with $T_2^\ast$.

Microscopically, for each proton:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \phi_{inhom}(t)&=\Delta \omega_{inhom} \cdot t \\
\Delta \phi_{spin-spin}(t)&=\Delta \omega_{spin-spin} \cdot t
\end{aligned}
$$

Macroscopically, for the transverse magnetization:

$$
M_{xy}(t)=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2}}\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_{2,inhom}}}=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2^*}}
$$

After the 180-degree pulse, true $T_2$ decay continues, but the phase spread from static field inhomogeneity begins to refocus. This is the same idea as the common shuttle-sprint analogy: the faster runners are sent to the back, and then they catch up.

Microscopically, for each proton:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Delta \phi_{inhom}(t)&=\Delta \omega_{inhom} \cdot |t-TE| \\
\Delta \phi_{spin-spin}(t)&=\Delta \omega_{spin-spin} \cdot t
\end{aligned}
$$

Macroscopically, for the transverse magnetization:

$$
M_{xy}(t)=M_0\cdot e^{-\frac{t}{T_2}}\cdot e^{-\frac{|t-TE|}{T_{2,inhom}}}
$$

## How Sinc RF Pulse Works under Slice Selection Gradient

The useful conclusion is this: for a symmetric 90-degree slice-selective RF pulse, the pulse behaves approximately like an instantaneous flip at the middle of the RF pulse. Therefore, after the pulse, we need a negative slice-rephasing gradient whose area is half of the positive slice-selection gradient area. The reason is that the transverse magnetization effectively starts accumulating slice-dependent phase from the middle of the RF pulse, not from the beginning.

For a 180-degree pulse, that extra negative rephasing lobe is not needed in the same way. If the 180-degree pulse is treated as a sudden flip at the middle of a symmetric slice-selection gradient, the phase accumulated before the midpoint is conjugated by the 180-degree pulse and cancels the phase accumulated after the midpoint.

The key point is that protons excited near the beginning of the RF pulse, at $t=-T/2$, experience a long period of gradient-driven dephasing before the end of the pulse. Protons excited near the end of the RF pulse, at $t=T/2$, experience almost no such dephasing.

Assume a constant slice-selection gradient $G_{SS}$ during the RF pulse, and place the center of the RF pulse at $t=0$. A spin at position $z$ has off-resonance angular frequency

$$
\Delta\omega(z)=\gamma G_{SS}z
$$

Under the small-tip-angle approximation, the contribution created by $B_1(t)$ is rotated into the transverse plane at time $t$, then accumulates phase until the pulse ends at $T/2$. Therefore

$$
M_{xy}(z) \approx i\gamma M_0 \int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} B_1(t)
e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}z\left(\frac{T}{2}-t\right)}\,\mathrm{d}t
$$

The phase term

$$
\phi(t,z)=-\gamma G_{SS}z\left(\frac{T}{2}-t\right)
$$

is the phase accumulated between the moment a small amount of transverse magnetization is created at time $t$ and the end of the pulse. Rearranging the integral gives

$$
M_{xy}(z) \approx i\gamma M_0 e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}zT/2}
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}} B_1(t)e^{i\gamma G_{SS}zt}\,\mathrm{d}t
$$

This shows the central fact: apart from a linear phase factor, the slice profile is the Fourier transform of the RF waveform $B_1(t)$, evaluated at spatial frequency $\gamma G_{SS}z$.

There is a nice symmetry hidden inside this integral. Split the exponential into two parts:

$$
e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}z\left(\frac{T}{2}-t\right)}
=e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}zT/2}e^{i\gamma G_{SS}zt}
$$

The first factor is independent of the integration variable $t$, so it can be pulled outside the integral. The second factor stays inside the integral:

$$
M_{xy}(z) \approx i\gamma M_0 e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}zT/2}
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)e^{i\gamma G_{SS}zt}\,\mathrm{d}t
$$

For a sinc RF pulse centered at $t=0$, the envelope is even:

$$
B_1(t)=B_1(-t)
$$

Using Euler's formula,

$$
e^{i\gamma G_{SS}zt}=\cos(\gamma G_{SS}zt)+i\sin(\gamma G_{SS}zt)
$$

so the integral becomes

$$
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)\cos(\gamma G_{SS}zt)\,\mathrm{d}t
+i\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)\sin(\gamma G_{SS}zt)\,\mathrm{d}t
$$

Now the "miracle" is just parity. $B_1(t)$ is even, $\cos(\gamma G_{SS}zt)$ is even, and $\sin(\gamma G_{SS}zt)$ is odd. Therefore $B_1(t)\cos(\gamma G_{SS}zt)$ is even, while $B_1(t)\sin(\gamma G_{SS}zt)$ is odd. The odd part integrates to zero over the symmetric interval $[-T/2,T/2]$:

$$
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)\sin(\gamma G_{SS}zt)\,\mathrm{d}t=0
$$

So the integral is purely real:

$$
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)e^{i\gamma G_{SS}zt}\,\mathrm{d}t
=
\int_{-\frac{T}{2}}^{\frac{T}{2}}B_1(t)\cos(\gamma G_{SS}zt)\,\mathrm{d}t
$$

This real value is the slice-profile amplitude. All of the complicated asymmetric phase caused by the finite RF pulse width has cancelled inside the symmetric integral, leaving only the fixed linear phase term:

$$
M_{xy}(z)\approx i\gamma M_0\cdot(\text{slice-profile amplitude})\cdot e^{-i\gamma G_{SS}zT/2}
$$

Physically, the remaining phase factor means this: the continuous excitation behaves as if all the transverse magnetization were created instantaneously at the pulse center $t=0$, and then freely dephased under the slice-selection gradient for another $T/2$.

This is the mathematical reason that a symmetric 90-degree RF pulse can be treated like an instantaneous flip at its midpoint.
